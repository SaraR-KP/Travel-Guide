Dubrovnik

About

Nestled in the southern part of Croatia, Dubrovnik is renowned for its rich historical heritage. Founded in the 7th century by Latins seeking refuge from the Avars and Slavs, the city has a storied past. From the 9th to the 12th century, Dubrovnik defended itself while acknowledging Venetian suzerainty, maintaining a degree of independence. In 1420, it was sold to Venice along with the rest of Dalmatia.

Dubrovnik's golden age flourished in the 15th and 16th centuries, when it became a vibrant hub for the exchange of goods such as salt, wine, textiles, and wool. Today, the city is celebrated as a UNESCO World Heritage site, renowned for its stunning beauty and rich cultural history.

Visit Old Town

Since 1979 the Old Town or also known as Stari Grad of Dubrovnik has been on UNESCO's list of World Heritage Sites. Situated on a small peninsula within the larger city of Dubrovnik it is famous for its well-preserved medieval walls, there you can see the Lovrjenac tower which is located outside the western edge of Dubrovnik’s city walls, The Sponza Palace (a 16th-century palace), multiple squares, like Stradun and Gundulic square and many other monuments, old ports, churches and monasteries and last but not least Orlando's pillar.

Rector's Palace

The Rector's Palace in Dubrovnik is a historic Gothic-Renaissance building that once served as the administrative seat of the Dubrovnik Republic. Built in the 15th century, it housed the rector's office, public halls, and government offices. The palace features elegant architecture, blending Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque elements due to numerous reconstructions after fires and earthquakes. Today, it functions as a museum, showcasing period furniture, paintings, and artifacts that reflect Dubrovnik's rich political and cultural history.

Take a look in the Red History Museum

The Red History Museum in Dubrovnik offers a unique insight into Croatia's socialist past, focusing on the period when the country was part of Yugoslavia (1945-1991). Through interactive exhibits, the museum showcases everyday life under the socialist regime, featuring objects, propaganda, and stories that reflect the political and social changes of the era.

Visitors can explore themes such as industry, culture, surveillance, and resistance, gaining a deeper understanding of how this period shaped modern Croatian society. The museum provides an immersive experience for those interested in learning about this often overlooked chapter in history.
